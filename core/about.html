Stocks, Excel, and HTML in Grade School

In the 4th grade, we were tasked with picking stocks and updating their prices on a large hand-written chart. Each day started with a lot of excitement as we looked up our prices and calculated our imaginary balances. I was so intrigued with it all — the numbers, the data, the charts — that I started going to the library during recess to research stocks. I also started saving my lunch money so that I could buy penny stocks.

Learning Excel also fascinated me. Seeing everything I had previously calculated by pencil and paper being instantly computed felt like magic to me.

I also started going to the pc lab after school. There was no club or agenda, just a teacher as a chaperone and other students doing homework or leisurely browsing the web. With the help of the teacher and other students, I learned HTML and how to create basic websites. This also amazed me.

Finance and Fundraising after College

After studying economics in college, I moved to NYC to work in finance, and then spent some time working in fundraising for my alma mater. I had always been referred to as the Excel-guy and found myself drawn to the data aspects of my finance and fundraising roles. While creating reports, I would investigate discrepancies and figure out how I could make the data more reliable in order to make the reporting process more seamless.

Data Today

Most issues I explored left me wanting to look further than my role permitted. So I eventually shifted to data-centric roles and took on validation, migration, ETL, and integration. I also do modeling for reporting layers and apps.

Data Tomorrow

I'm currently learning to build REST APIs using Swift Vapor and how to self-host from macOS. From there I'll develop native macOS apps and make them available remotely through web browsers, similar to cloud gaming. Streaming native apps should provide greater performance and security than client-side web apps, and could be a great option for companies returning to on-prem and zero-trust solutions.
